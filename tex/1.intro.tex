\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}


Recent advances in foundation models have substantially reshaped research and applications in Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Computer Vision (CV). In contrast, the development of similarly mature adaptation paradigms for graph-structured data remains comparatively limited \cite{
devlin2019bert,
brown2020language, liu2023pretrain,

% li2022blip,

wang2023chatvideo,
zhang2023videollama}. This gap is important because graphs are a natural abstraction for relational and structured data, and they play a central role in applications such as molecular discovery, drug design, recommender systems, and scientific modeling \cite{liu2023graph, zhang2023large, yang2023datacentric, rong2020selfsupervised, qian2023can, wang2023scientific}. As a result, there is growing interest in general-purpose graph learning systems that can better transfer across tasks, domains, and modalities.

However, achieving this goal remains difficult. Figure \ref{fig:AGIProblems} highlights three recurring technical challenges for graph learning research. \textbf{\textit{(1) How can models generalize across modalities?}} Recent multimodal foundation models have shown strong capabilities in aligning and reasoning across textual and visual inputs \cite{
devlin2019bert,

% li2022blip,
% zhu2023minigpt4,
zhang2023videollama,
brown2020language}. In graph-centered settings, however, comparable progress remains much more limited, especially when graph structure must be integrated with textual, visual, or scientific signals \cite{li2023graphadapter}.

\textbf{\textit{(2) How can models generalize across domains?}} Transfer learning has been effective in many machine learning settings, but transferring knowledge between graph domains remains challenging because both semantic spaces and structural patterns may shift substantially \cite{zhu2023graphcontrol, zhao2023graphglow}. This makes graph domain adaptation a difficult and still open research problem. \textbf{\textit{(3) How can models generalize across tasks?}} A large portion of the existing literature focuses on transferring pre-trained graph knowledge across tasks within the same graph domain, such as node classification, link prediction, and graph classification \cite{sun2022gppt, liu2023graphprompt, sun2023all, fang2023universal, zhu2023sglpt, hu2020gptgnn, shirkavand2023deep, ge2023enhancing}. Yet, compared with NLP and CV, standardized real-world evaluations and deployment-oriented studies for graph prompting remain relatively limited. Taken together, these observations suggest that graph prompting should be studied not only as a task adaptation trick, but also as part of a broader effort toward more general and reusable graph learning systems.
% Besides the above three foundation problems, Artificial General Intelligence has also encountered many social disputes. For example, training large foundation models consumes exorbitant amounts of energy and may yield unintended counterfactual outcomes \cite{liu2022ptuning, schick2021it}.


These challenges have made parameter-efficient adaptation strategies increasingly attractive \cite{gao2021making, lester2021power}. Among them, prompt learning has emerged as a practical mechanism for adapting pre-trained models without repeatedly updating all model parameters \cite{qin2021learning, tsimpoukelli2021multimodal, liu2023pretrain}. Prompting modifies the input or task formulation so that downstream problems better align with behaviors induced during pre-training. In many settings, this enables practitioners to reuse pre-trained models with substantially fewer trainable parameters than full fine-tuning \cite{brown2020language, jiang2020how, lester2021power, shin2020autoprompt}. For graph learning, this property makes prompting a promising, though not universally sufficient, mechanism for task adaptation, cross-domain transfer, and multimodal interaction.

From a representational perspective, several data modalities can be reinterpreted through graph-structured abstractions. A sentence may be modeled as a dependency graph or token interaction graph, and an image may be represented as a grid graph or region graph. This perspective motivates the adaptation of successful prompting ideas from language and vision to graph learning \cite{sun2022gppt, liu2023graphprompt, sun2023all, fang2023universal, zhu2023sglpt, ma2023hetgpt, guo2023datacentric, chen2023ultradp, gong2023prompt}.


Although graph prompting is partly inspired by prompting techniques in NLP, the two are not directly analogous \cite{sun2023all}. \textbf{First}, the design space of graph prompts is substantially richer. In language models, prompts may take the form of discrete templates, soft prompt vectors, prefixes, or instruction-style conditioning \cite{brown2020language, gao2021making}. In graph learning, however, a prompt often also involves structural design choices, insertion mechanisms, and answering functions, rather than prompt content alone. \textbf{Second}, aligning downstream graph problems with pre-training objectives is typically more difficult than in language tasks \cite{liu2023graphprompt, sun2023all}. A typical language pre-training task such as masked word prediction \cite{devlin2019bert} shares a relatively direct interface with many downstream tasks \cite{liu2023pretrain}, whereas graph tasks span node-level \cite{grover2016node2vec}, edge-level \cite{zhang2018link}, and graph-level objectives \cite{sun2020infograph, sun2021heterogeneous}, making pre-training pretexts less directly reusable. \textbf{Third}, graph prompts are usually less interpretable to non-specialists than natural-language prompts, because their effects are mediated by graph topology, feature transformations, and message passing. These differences make it necessary to examine graph prompting on its own terms rather than treating it as a straightforward extension of NLP prompting.




The literature on graph prompting has expanded rapidly in recent years, but it remains fragmented across task settings, graph modalities, and adaptation strategies. Existing works often emphasize different perspectives, making direct comparison difficult and slowing the systematic accumulation of knowledge in this area. This survey therefore provides a structured review of graph prompting through the lens of the three transfer challenges discussed above, while also clarifying its relationship to graph pre-training, graph foundation models, and multimodal graph learning. To organize the field, we consider the following research questions (RQs):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1: How can existing graph prompting methods be organized under a unified analytical framework?} We seek a common vocabulary for comparing methods that differ in prompt representation, insertion mechanism, and adaptation objective.
   \item  \textbf{RQ2: What functional role do prompts play in graph representation learning?} We study what graph prompts actually contribute, how they interact with graph learners, and why they may behave differently from conventional fine-tuning.


   \item  \textbf{RQ3: What are the main design dimensions of graph prompts and prompt tuning strategies?} We examine how graph prompts are constructed, how they align downstream tasks with pre-training objectives, and how they are optimized in practice.





   \item  \textbf{RQ4: How can graph prompting be supported in practical applications and software ecosystems?} We discuss the current state of toolkits, benchmarks, and reusable implementations for graph prompting in real-world scenarios.


   \item  \textbf{RQ5: What limitations remain in current graph prompting research, and which future directions appear most promising?} We summarize open challenges and outline research directions that may shape the next stage of the field.


\end{itemize}




RQ1 motivates a unified framework for analyzing graph prompt learning. This framework decomposes graph prompts into prompt tokens, token structures, and inserting patterns, thereby providing a stable lens for comparing existing variants.


RQ2 concerns the relationship between prompts and existing graph models through the lenses of flexibility and expressiveness, leading to a deeper understanding of the role of graph prompts. Unlike many prompt learning surveys in NLP \cite{liu2023pretrain}, which treat prompting primarily as a mechanism for bridging pre-training and downstream tasks, we discuss graph prompting as being intertwined with the modeling capacity of graph learners themselves. This perspective helps clarify why prompting is promising in the graph domain and how it differs from conventional fine-tuning methods \cite{hu2020strategies}.


RQ3 motivates a taxonomy that organizes representative graph prompting studies according to node-level, edge-level, and graph-level settings, while aligning them with the broader context of pre-training objectives. This organization clarifies prompt mechanisms within the overall ``pre-training and prompting'' workflow.

RQ4 concerns the supporting ecosystem for graph prompting. We discuss toolkits such as ProG (prompt graph) \footnote{\url{https://github.com/sheldonresearch/ProG}} and curated repositories\footnote{\url{https://github.com/WxxShirley/Awesome-Graph-Prompt}} that improve accessibility, reproducibility, and comparability for researchers and practitioners working on graph prompting.

Beyond these questions, the survey further discusses application scenarios, current challenges, and future research directions, thereby outlining a broader roadmap for the development of graph prompt learning (RQ5). The contributions are summarised as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Enabling Comprehensive Analysis.} We develop a unified framework for analyzing graph prompt learning, providing a structured view of prompt tokens, token structures, and inserting patterns.
    \item \textbf{Prompt--Model Perspective. } We discuss graph prompts not only as task adapters but also as mechanisms that interact with the flexibility and expressiveness of graph learners.
    \item \textbf{A Systematic Taxonomy of Graph Prompting. } We synthesize representative graph prompting studies and organize them within the overarching ``pre-training and prompting'' workflow.
    % \item \textbf{Quantitative and Qualitative Insights. } We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate different graph prompts and propose a new metric to evaluate how well a graph prompt reformulates graph data.
    \item \textbf{Empowering the Graph Prompting Ecosystem. } We summarize toolkits and community resources that support reproducible graph prompting research.
    \item \textbf{Charting a Path Forward. } We provide a detailed discussion of current challenges and future directions in the field.
\end{itemize}
