\section{Survey Methodology}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Research Objectives}

This survey studies graph prompting as a route toward more transferable and reusable graph learning systems. We organize the discussion around the three transfer challenges summarized in Table \ref{tab:rqs}: transfer across modalities, domains, and tasks. Prompting has become an important adaptation mechanism in NLP and vision \cite{brown2020language, gao2021making,
% radford2021learning,
wang2023chatvideo}, but its role in graph learning is still less settled. Our goal is therefore not to assume that prompting is a universal solution, but to examine when and how graph prompts help graph models transfer across tasks and domains, and how they interact with other modalities such as text and images. Beyond these shared transfer challenges, graph prompting also differs from its counterparts in NLP and CV, which motivates the more specific research questions listed in Table \ref{tab:rqs}.

\subsection{Taxonomy}

Figure \ref{fig:Taxonomy} summarizes how this survey organizes the graph prompting literature. The taxonomy is designed to show how prompt-based methods relate to pre-training objectives, transfer settings, and application scenarios in graph learning.



\textbf{(1) Pre-training Strategies for Graph Prompt.}

Prompting methods are often motivated by the need to align downstream tasks with behaviors induced during pre-training. For this reason, the survey first reviews representative pre-training strategies in graph learning before turning to prompting itself. We group existing pre-training approaches into node-level, edge-level, graph-level, and multi-task settings, and then examine how later prompting methods reuse or adapt these objectives. This setup makes it easier to compare prompting methods against the pre-training assumptions on which they depend.



\textbf{(2) Prompting Methods in Graph Learning.}

To cover the three transfer challenges defined in Table \ref{tab:rqs}, we analyze graph prompting from three directions: \romannumeral1. prompt design for graph tasks (\secref{sec:ptask}); \romannumeral2. multi-modal prompting with graphs (\secref{subsec:pmodal}); and \romannumeral3. graph domain adaptation with prompting techniques (\secref{subsec:pdomain}). Within each direction, we revisit the five detailed research questions in Table \ref{tab:rqs} and use them to connect method design, adaptation mechanism, and practical scope.

\textit{\romannumeral1. Prompt Design for Graph Tasks.} In this part, we introduce a unified analytical framework for graph prompt design. We organize existing methods around three components: \textit{prompt tokens}, which represent prompt content; \textit{token structures}, which specify how multiple prompt units are organized; and \textit{inserting patterns}, which determine how prompts are combined with the original graph. We further discuss answering functions, namely how downstream predictions are produced from prompted graphs, and summarize representative optimization strategies such as meta-learning, task-specific tuning, and pretext-aligned tuning. This organization lets us compare methods in terms of shared assumptions, design differences, and trade-offs.


\textit{\romannumeral2. Multi-modal Prompting with Graphs.} In this part, we discuss prompting methods for text-attributed and other multi-modal graph settings. The recent growth of graph-LLM and graph-language research has expanded this area substantially, so our goal here is not to provide a full survey of graph-LLM methods. Instead, we focus on prompting-oriented interfaces and adaptation mechanisms that explicitly involve graph structure.

\subsection{Literature Overview}

\subsection{Connection to Existing Work}

Recent surveys on graph prompt learning differ mainly in scope and organizing principle. \citet{wu2023survey} reviewed the early wave of graph prompting methods with an emphasis on techniques, applications, and open challenges when the methodological space was still relatively compact. \citet{long2024towards} expanded the discussion to design principles, application scenarios, benchmark issues, and prompt tuning strategies. \citet{fu2025graphprompting} organized the literature around the ``pre-train and prompt'' workflow and emphasized data-level, representation-level, and task-level perspectives.

Our emphasis differs in two respects. First, we adopt a unified decomposition of graph prompting into prompt tokens, token structures, insertion patterns, answering functions, and prompt tuning strategies. Second, we place graph prompting within a broader taxonomy that also includes pre-training strategies, multi-modal prompting, and graph domain adaptation. We further retain a practical perspective through the discussion of application scenarios and supporting toolkits such as ProG. Related surveys on graph foundation models, graph-LLM integration, and graph self-supervised learning \cite{liu2023graph, li2023survey, xie2023selfsupervised, yu2023selfsupervised} provide broader background, whereas this survey concentrates specifically on graph prompting and its surrounding adaptation mechanisms.
