\section{Pre-training GNNs for Graph Prompting}\label{sec:pretrain_method}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering

    \caption{Graph pre-training methods.}
    \label{fig:pretrain}
\end{figure}


Graph pre-training is a pivotal step of the pre-training, prompting, and predicting paradigm in graph representation learning. This approach leverages readily available information to encode the inherent graph structure, providing a robust foundation for generalization across diverse downstream tasks. By integrating these pre-training methods into the comprehensive workflow, we offer an exploration of their interplay with the subsequent prompting and predicting phases, shedding light on the strengths and limitations of this holistic approach. 
This unique perspective distinguishes our survey, framing graph pre-training as an integral part of the broader graph-prompting learning process.
To better illustrate the motivation behind the prompting paradigm, we will now delve into four distinct pre-training strategies within the traditional pre-training and fine-tuning framework.



\subsection{Node-level Strategies}\label{subsec:node_pre}
Node-level pre-training methods aim to learn transferable local representations for nodes and their neighborhoods. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pretrain}, these methods are usually built around either contrastive or predictive objectives. In contrastive settings, self-supervised signals are typically created by perturbing graph structure or node attributes and then maximizing agreement between the original and augmented views. Representative node-level contrastive methods include~\cite{cheng2023wiener, zhu2021graph, jin2021multiscale, peng2020graph, jiang2021pretraining, wang2021selfsuperviseda}.

Predictive node-level methods instead reconstruct masked or corrupted information from the unperturbed graph. Representative examples include masked autoencoding and related reconstruction objectives~\cite{hou2022graphmae, wang2017mgae, park2019symmetric, wang2021selfsupervised, hou2023graphmae2}. These methods can learn useful local patterns, but their transferability still depends on how well the pretext objective matches the downstream task. In particular, methods centered on local corruption or reconstruction may under-emphasize long-range dependencies or graph-level semantics. Recent work has also extended node-oriented pre-training to multi-graph settings, which is more consistent with the graph foundation model view of transfer~\cite{lachi2024graphfm}.


\subsection{Edge-level Strategies}\label{subsec:edge_pre}
Edge-level pre-training focuses on node interactions and pairwise relations, which makes it particularly relevant to tasks such as link prediction. One common approach is to discriminate whether an edge should exist between a pair of nodes, which can be viewed as a contrastive or relational discrimination objective~\cite{jin2021node, long2022pretraining}. Another approach reconstructs masked or corrupted edges, often through adjacency recovery or related generative objectives~\cite{tan2023s2gae, li2023what, pan2018adversarially, hasanzadeh2019semiimplicit, kim2021how}.

These strategies are useful when downstream performance depends strongly on local connectivity patterns. Their limitation is that they are usually centered on structure alone. As a result, they may not transfer well when node attributes, higher-order semantics, or graph-level objectives matter more than pairwise edge recovery. Recent benchmark work likewise evaluates transfer more broadly than edge reconstruction alone~\cite{xu2024graphfm}.


\subsection{Graph-level Strategies}\label{subsec:graph_pre}
Graph-level pre-training aims to improve whole-graph representations, especially for downstream tasks defined on subgraphs or complete graph instances. Similar to node- and edge-level settings, graph-level strategies are often divided into reconstruction-based and contrastive approaches. Reconstruction-based methods mask graph components and learn to recover them~\cite{xie2022selfsupervised, rong2020selfsupervised}. Contrastive methods instead maximize mutual information between local and global views~\cite{velickovic2019deep, sun2020infograph, hassani2020contrastive, sun2021mocl, subramonian2021motifdriven}, or distinguish positive and negative graph pairs~\cite{you2020graph, suresh2021adversarial, thakoor2021bootstrapped, qiu2020gcc}.

These methods often produce useful graph-level representations, especially when the downstream objective is close to the pretext task. The main difficulty is transfer across tasks and datasets with larger semantic gaps. When the pre-training signal is too narrowly aligned with one kind of graph structure, the resulting encoder may transfer poorly and can even suffer from negative transfer~\cite{rosenstein2005transfer}. More recent work has pushed graph-level pre-training toward cross-domain and text-attributed graph settings, which broadens the scope of transfer beyond a single graph family~\cite{he2024unigraph, song2024pure}.


\subsection{Multi-task Pre-training}\label{subsec:multi_pre}
Multi-task pre-training combines several objectives so that the encoder learns from multiple views of graph data at once. Typical choices include joint optimization of attribute reconstruction and structural recovery~\cite{hu2020strategies, hu2020gptgnn, zhang2020graphbert, fang2022geometryenhanced}. For example, Hu et al.~\cite{hu2020strategies} pre-trained a GNN at both the node and graph levels so that local and global information could be learned together. Other work combines contrastive learning across multiple granularities~\cite{jiang2021contrastive, xu2021infogcl}.

The advantage of multi-task pre-training is breadth: the encoder is less tied to a single pretext task. The drawback is optimization complexity. Different objectives may compete with each other, and the resulting model may underperform on some tasks even if aggregate pre-training loss improves. For this reason, multi-task pre-training is best understood as a broader design space rather than a guaranteed remedy for negative transfer. This line of thinking now overlaps with multi-graph and multi-domain pre-training, where the goal is to build encoders that transfer across graph collections rather than within a single benchmark only~\cite{lachi2024graphfm, yu2025samgpt}.


\subsection{Further Discussion} \label{subsec:pre-train-dis}
The above strategies show that graph pre-training can provide reusable encoders, but they also clarify why pre-training alone is not sufficient for broad transfer. A pre-trained model is useful only when its learned representations and pretext objectives remain compatible with downstream tasks, domains, and graph structures. Prompting offers one way to reduce this mismatch: instead of fully re-optimizing the encoder for every new setting, a graph prompt can adjust the input graph, task interface, or readout process so that the downstream problem is closer to the behavior induced during pre-training. This does not make prompting a universal replacement for fine-tuning, since its effectiveness still depends on prompt design, pre-training diversity, and the size of the distribution shift. It does, however, provide a practical adaptation mechanism for settings where supervision is limited or repeated full fine-tuning is expensive.
